First off, let us distinguish between the what and the how.

Let us say we intend to embark on a study of the liver. The dialectical way to go about it is to first (a)define the liver, the what, and then (b)study the how, that is the inner workings of the liver or how the liver works and to what end.

Our being able to formulate a valid definition does not mean we know the inner workings of the concept defined.

The definition of the liver might be a couple of lines long but its inner workings are hunderds of thousands, if not millions, lines long.

However, despite the huge difference in the quantity of text between the what and the how, they are equally important, especially when the concept defined does not have a visual representation like, for example, Justice.

The definition serves to bring into sharp focus the object of our study. If our definition is wrong, we will end up studying some other concept instead. If, for example, our definition of Justice were really the definition of punishment, then we would be studying punishment instead of Justice. Therefore, the stakes involved in formulating a valid definition are really high.

A WORD ON CONCEPTS • EN KOMMENTER OM BEGREBER

Literally everything that exists, be it animate or inanimate, is, among other things, a concept. Definitions describe concepts. All concepts have at least one representation, be it physical, visual, literal, or conceptual.

For instance, an apple, a concrete noun, can be represented in 4 objective ways:

  1. physical (real apples)

  2. visual (on a screen or in a photo or in a painting):

  3. lexical (the word): apple

  4. conceptual (definition): An apple is typically round or oblong fruit with smooth or slightly textured skin. The skin color can vary depending on the variety and can be shades of red, green, yellow, or a combination thereof. Apples have a firm and juicy flesh and their taste can range from sweet to tart.

But kindness, an abstract noun, can be represented only in 2 objective ways:

  1. lexical (the word): kindness

  2. conceptual (definition): def. 7 Kindness is a positive quality or characteristic demonstrated through compassionate and considerate behavior towards others.

The thing to understand here is that (a)since representations of concepts are something and (b)since everything is, among other things, a concept, (c)it follows that representations of concepts are also concepts.

We can therefore refer to (a)the concept of the physical representation of an apple or (b)the concept of the lexical representation of an apple and so on.

Some concepts are universal while others are not. For example, lexical representations, that is words or vocabulary, are not universal because they are language dependent, while conceptual represenations are universal. Indeed, the lexical representation of an in English is apple, while in Danish is æble; however, the conceptual representation of an apple, that is its definitions, is universal.

So, what is truth?

def. 12 Truth is what happens or takes place without exception. (Isidoros Parlamas 1985)

Let me expain by example.

dialog 1
1. Plato — Is it true that the sun rises in the east?
2. Socrates — No.
3. Plato — Why not?
4. Socrates — Because the statement is not devoid of exceptions.
5. Plato — How so?
6. Socrates — Well, on planet Venus, the sun rises in the west.
7. Plato — Is it true that on planet Eart the sun rises in the east?

Truth 1 (a)Parts of Speech are concepts. (b)There are 10 Parts of Speech and (c)each word belongs in at least one of them. Parts of Speech are (d)language independent, meaning that they are (e)lexicon independent, (f)punctuation independent, (g)syntax independent, (h)morphology* independent, and (i)periphrases** independent.

A verb, then, remains a verb in all languages regardless of the lexicon or punctuation or syntax or morphology or periphrasis involved.

You really   need to understand that while lexicon, syntax, punctuation, morphology, and periphrasis are language dependent, concepts, and by extension, Parts of Speech, are not. That is to say that Parts of Speech are universal.

*morphology refers to internal structure and formation of words in a language, which involves, among other things, prefixes, suffixes, inflection, declension, conjugation, and periphrases. In English, we decline nouns, adjectives, and pronouns and we conjugate verbs. For instance, the subject of verbs in English is indicated by nouns or subject pronouns: sent. 8 Helen works or she works, but in Spanish, it is indicated by the ending of the verb as the verb is conjugated: Helena trabaja or ella trabaja or trabaja; Helena y Juan trabajan or ellos trabajan or trabajan.

**perifrasis, plural periphrases, is part of morphology and refers to polylexical (multi-worded) forms of Parts of Speech. For example, to be fond of is a periphrasis of to like, but they are both verbs and their meaning is roughly the same. Even though is a periphrasis of although, but they are both conjunctions and their meaning is roughly the same. Periphrases do not always occur in pairs. For example, look forward to does not have a monolexical (single-worded) counterpart.

For instance, working hard is a concept. sent. 9 They work hard is expressed in all countries, irrespective of lexicon, punctuation, syntax, morphology or periphrases. So, on planet Earth, in this specific example, they will always be a subject pronoun, work will always be a verb, and hard will always be an adverb.

Let us see this same sentence in a few different languages:

We can see that in every single language above, fuchsia is the subject pronoun, blue is the verb and red is the adverb, which demonstrates neatly that Parts of Speech are universal.

Parts of Speech are, then, strictly function dependent, which means that each Part of Speech plays a distinct nonvariable role in the clause; in other words, the role of verbs is invariably the same, the role of nouns is invariably the same, and so on.

The role that each Part of Speech has in clauses is spelled out in its own definition, meaning the definition of each Part of Speech is really the description of its role in the clause.

This work became one of the foundational texts in the field of Greek grammar and had a significant influence on subsequent grammatical studies in both Greek and Latin; it is still regarded as the groundwork of the entire Western grammatical tradition and beyond.

In "The Art of Grammar," Dionysius the Thrax described the parts of speech in Greek including onomata (nouns and adjectives), verbs, participles, articles, pronouns, prepositions, adverbs, and conjunctions. He also discussed topics such as syntax (sentence structure), and morphology (word formation).

Dionysius the Thrax's work has had a lasting impact on the study of grammar worldwide. His classification of the Parts of Speech and his grammatical analysis methods became fundamental principles in subsequent grammatical studies and influenced the development of grammatical theories in most languages.

The Parts of Speech and their definitions are universal, which means that all languages have exactly the same Parts of Speech.

However, grammarians are notoriously inaccurate and will tell you that different languages have different Parts of Speech, which is totally untrue.

What they really mean is that Parts of Speech in different languages may consist of different or more or fewer types or classes or subcategories. For example, they will tell you that English has 1 class of adjectives while Japanese has 3.

It is true that some languages, such as Russian, lack articles, but the remaining Parts of Speech are the same ones as in every other language.

But why are The Parts of Speech important?

Because each one of them defines a distinct role/function of words in clauses and, because of that, they help us study and learn languages infinitely more efficiently.

The definition of each Part of Speech spells out the role/function of each Part of Speech in a clause and those definitions are formulated on the basis of universal conceptual criteria. That means that if a word satisfies the definition of verbs in a given clause, then it is a verb regardless of language, puncuation, syntax, morphology, or any periphrases involved. (Truth 1 on page 4-5)

def. 23 A clause is a word or a group of words which, as a minimum, contains a subject and a verb.

def. 24a A main clause is a clause which can stand on its own.

def. 24b A main clause is a clause which contains no conjunctions.

def. 25a A subordinate clause is a clause that cannot stand on its own.

def. 25b A subordinate clause is a clause which contains a conjunction.

def. 26 A sentence is either a lone main clause or a mixture of main and subordinate clauses.

def. 20 Conjunctions are words or groups of words that connect clauses while, at the same time, establish fundamental relationships between them. (page 8)

Remember that definitions are not formulated just to waste paper and ink but rather to be religiously observed.

If definitions are not observed, their very purpose is defeated and chaos ensues.

Ridiculously, in the overwhelming majority of grammar books that I have come across, authors repeatedly ignore their own definitions, which betrays their mechanical or robotic attitude towards authoring.


This is the top part of page 3 of a Danish grammar "Skriverguiden" titled "At Skrive" authored by Fanny Slotorub. It is a guide to another book by the same title and author on "Tekstsammenhæng og argumentation," ISBN 978-87-636-0418-5, published by "Praxis."

These 2 books contain a plethora of errors, all because of one single but very important misunderstanding, which we are going to unravel in depth in the following paragraphs.

The books in question are written in Danish and the author defines a main clause as a clause that can stand on its own, def. 24a, which is correct.

In the book, as you can see in the screenshot above, the following 2 clauses are cited as examples of a main clause: dansk (a)Vi spiser. and dansk (b) For vi er sultne. ( example 8 We eat. / example 9 For we are hungry.), for here meaning because.

Here, the author ignores her own definition since example 9 cannot stand on its own and, therefore, by definition, it does not qualify as a main clause.

Actually, example 9 is a subordinate clause not just in English and Danish, but in all languages.

But what exactly does can stand on its own / dansk kan stå alene mean in the definition of a main clause?

It means that no additional information is necessary for a clause to make sense.

Let us consider these 2 dialogs:

dialog 2

Son — Good morning! For we are hungry.

Mother — What!?

In dialog 1, mother is confused because her son is uttering only a cause, but not an effect, so it all sounds gibberish; for is a cause-and-effect or aetiological conjunction and it introduces an aetiological clause. Aetiological clauses are subordinate clauses and require additional information in order for them to make sense. (aetiological or etiological)

• • •

dialog 3

Son — Good morning! We are hungry.

Mother — Well, make some breakfast.

In dialog 3, communication flows smoothly.

It is obvious that example 9 cannot stand on its own and therefore it is a subordinate clause. Period!

So, what is going on?

Let me explain.

In Danish, in principle, the syntax of main clauses happens to be Subject + Verb + Adverb or SVA.

For instance, dansk ViS arbejderV hårdt.A / example 10 WeS workV hard.A

Now, the syntax of clauses introduced by for also happens to be SVA, that is the same syntax as that of main clauses.

For instance, dansk For vejretS erV ogsåA dårligt. / example 11 For the weatherS isV alsoA bad.

Voilà! The incredibly superficial logic emerges!

It goes like this:

Since the syntax of the clauses introduced by for is the same as the syntax of main clauses, it follows that clauses introduced by for are also main clauses, which of course is nonsense.

Never mind that clause example 11 cannot stand on its own, which is the opposite of the very definition of main clauses the author herself cites on page 3 in her own book. That is all forgotten now, which betrays the mechanical or robotic mindset of most authors.

The author's logic would be OK if the definition of main clauses were Main clauses are clauses whose syntax is SVA, which, of course, is not the definition of main clauses.

To correct the contradiction, hovedsætningskonjuktioner / main-clause conjunctions should be renamed SVA conjunctions.

stående regel 1 Hovedsætningskonjuktioner (og, men, eller, for, så) introducerer hovedsætninger.

standing rule 1 Main-clause-conjunctions (og, men, eller, for, så) introduce main clauses.

standing rule 1 is, of course, nonsense because it violates Truth 2 on page 13.

The new rule, then, would be formulated as follows:

ny regle 1 SVA konjuktioner (og, men, eller, for, så) introducerer SVA ledsætninger.

new rule 1 SVA conjunctions (og, men, eller, for, så) introduce SVA subordinate clauses.

point 6 - Simil. Both in English and Danish, we have a sentence which consists of (a)1 subordinate clause and (b)1 main clause.

point 7 - Diff. ?

Rel. The relationship here is a resultative one or an aetiological one. That is to say, the two clauses are bound by a relationship of cause and effect or cause and result, the first clause being the cause and the second the result.

In this example, reversing the order of the clauses does not work in either language:

He paid for everyone's drinks, he felt so happy (it does not work)

At han betalte for alles drinks, han følte sig så glad. (it does not work)

In both languages, then, the order is (a)cause first and (b)effect second.

Conclusion: In English and Danish, the conjunctions ...so...that... • ...så...at... introduce clauses of cause-and-effect and the clauses work in a very similar fashion. After all, both English and Danish belong to the Germanic family of languages...

point 8 - Simil. In both English and Danish, we have a sentence which consists of (a)1 subordinate clause and (b)1 main clause.

point 9 - Simil. Both in English and Danish, reversing the order of the clauses works.

point 10 - Diff. Danes love commas more than English people, so they use a comma irrespective of the order of the clauses; the English, on the other hand, in general, but not always, use a comma only when the subordinate clause comes first.

point 11 - Diff. When the subordinate clause comes first, the English use comma, while in Danish, the main clause undergoes inversion (first the verb, then the subject).

Rel. The relationship here one of contrast or of unexpectedness or against the norm. That is to say, the two clauses are bound by a relationship of break from the norm or contrasting realities.

Conclusion: In English and Danish, the conjunctions although • selvom introduce clauses of contast.
