A WORD ON CONCEPTS • EN KOMMENTER OM BEGREBER

Literally everything that exists, be it animate or inanimate, is, among other things, a concept. Definitions describe concepts. All concepts have a representation, be it physical, visual, literal, or conceptual.

For instance, an apple, a concrete noun, can be represented in 4 objective ways:

  1. physical (real apples)

  2. visual (on a screen or in a photo or in a painting):

  3. literal (the word): apple

  4. conceptual (definition): An apple is typically round or oblong fruit with smooth or slightly textured skin. The skin color can vary depending on the variety and can be shades of red, green, yellow, or a combination thereof. Apples have a firm and juicy flesh and their taste can range from sweet to tart.

But kindness, an abstract noun, can be represented only in 2 objective ways:

  1. literal (the word): kindness

  2. conceptual (definition): Kindness is a positive quality or characteristic demonstrated through compassionate and considerate behavior towards others.

The thing to understand here is that (a)since represenations of concepts are something and (b)since everything is, among other things, a concept, (c)it follows that representations of concepts are also concepts.

We can therefore refer to the concept of the physical representation of an apple or the concept of the literal representation of an apple and so on.

Some concepts are universal while others are not. For example, literal representations, that is words, are not universal, while conceptual represenations are universal. Indeed, the literal representation of an in English is apple, while in Danish is æble; however, conceptual representations are universal.

A WORD ON LANGUAGE • EN KOMMENTER OM SPROG

A language can be broken down into (a)vocabulary (words), (b)phonetics (pronunciation) rules, (c)punctuation rules, (d)syntax (word order) rules, and (e)morphology rules, part of which are periphrases.

(f)There are far more concepts than words and (g)each word represents only one concept at a time and (h)each concept has a definition and (i)there is only one valid definition for each concept and (j)there is not a single word which represents only one concept.
(Isidoros Parlamas • Applied Dialectics & Horistics)

Let me elaborate on the statement above:

(f)There are far more concepts than words

For every word and in every language there are at least 2 concepts associated with it. As an example, let us think of water. How many concepts does water represent? Well, let's see how many of them we can think of off hand.

page 2

  1. I drink 2 liters of water every day. (H2O → uncountable noun)

    Jeg drikker 2 liter vand hver dag.

  2. My eyes water when I see homeless children. (intransitive verb)
    Mine øjne løber i vand, når jeg ser hjemløse børn.

  3. My mouth waters when I smell fresh fish. (intransitive verb)
    Min mund løber i vand, når jeg lugter frisk fisk.

  4. I water my garden once a week. (transitive verb)
    Jeg vander min have en gang om ugen.
We see that water represents at least 4 different concepts.

(g)Each word represents only one concept at a time

But even though water represents various concepts, it represents only one concept at a time. As you can see, in each of the 4 sentences above, water represents only one concept. For example, in sentence 1, it represents only H2O.

(h)each concept has a definition

The definition of water in sentence 1 above is its chemical formula: H2O.

The definition of water in sentence 2 above is overflowing of the eyes with tears.

The definition of water in sentence 3 above is the production of an abnormally large amount of saliva in the mouth.

The definition of water in sentence 4 above is providing moisture to plants and/or trees by applying water to the soil.

(i)there is only one valid definition for each concept

Of course, anyone can attempt to define a concept. If, for example, we ask 10 people to define justice, we will most probably end up with 10 different definitions, of which, most probably, none will be apposite.

Justice, just like all other words, represents more than one concept, but each specific concept of justice has only one valid definition.

It takes a lot of training in Dialectics and Horistics to be able to come up with valid definitions. Horistics is the art/skill of defining.

(j)there is not a single word which represents only one concept

This may sound impossible, but it is true. Even words such as and represent more than one concept. Let us take a look.

  1. Two and three equals five. (cummulative • and here conveys the concept of increase)

  2. George invited Helen to dinner, and he didn't pay for it. (contrast • and here means but)

  3. Jill was anxious about her date, and she canceled it. (effect • and here means so)

You get the idea...

PARTS OF SPEECH • DELE AF TALE

Important axiom: Parts of Speech are concepts. There are 10 Parts of Speech and each word belongs in one of them. Parts of Speech are language independent, meaning that they are (a)vocabulary independent, (b)punctuation independent, (c)syntax independent, (d)morphology* independent, and (e)periphrasis** independent. A verb, then, remains a verb in all languages regardless of the vocabulary or punctuationor syntax or morphology or periphrasis involved. You really   need to understand that while vocabulary, syntax,

page 3

punctuation, morphology, and (d)periphrasis are language dependent, concepts, and by extension, Parts of Speech, are not. That is to say that Parts of Speech are universal.

For instance, working hard is a concept. They work hard is said in all countries, irrespective of vocabulary, punctuation, word order, morphology or periphrases. So, on planet Earth, in this specific example, they will always be a subject pronoun, work will always be a verb, and hard will always be an adverb.

Let us see this same sentence in a few different languages:

  1. English:They work hard.

  2. Danish: De arbejder hårdt.

  3. Modern Greek: Αυτοί εργάζονται σκληρά.

  4. Spanish: Ellos trabajan duro.

  5. Polish: Oni pracują ciężko.

  6. Russian: Они много работают. (The syntax is different here, but Parts of Speech remain unaffected.)

  7. Mandarin Chinese: 他们 工作 努力。

We can see that in every single language above the fuchsia is the subject pronoun, the blue is the verb and the red is the adverb, which demonstrates neatly that Parts of Speech are universal.

Parts of Speech are, then, strictly function dependent, which means that each Part of Speech plays a distinct nonvariable role in the clause; in other words, the role of verbs is invariably the same, the role of nouns is invariably the same, and so on. The role that each Part of Speech has in clauses is spelled out in its own definition, meaning the definition of each Part of Speech is really the description of its role in the clause.

*morphology refers to internal structure and formation of words in a language, which involves, among other things, prefixes, suffixes, inflection, declension, conjugation, and periphrases. In English, we decline nouns, adjectives, and pronouns and we conjugate verbs. For instance, the subject of verbs in English is indicated by nouns or subject pronouns: Helen works or she works, but in Spanish, it is indicated by the different endings of the verb as the verb is conjugated: Helena trabaja or trabaja or ella trabaja; Helena y Juan trabajan or ellos trabajan.

**perifrasis, plural periphrases, is part of morphology and refers to polylexical (multi-worded) forms of Parts of Speech. For example, to be fond of is a periphrasis of to like, but they are both verbs and their meaning is roughly the same. Even though is a periphrasis of although, but they are both conjunctions and their meaning is roughly the same. Periphrases do not always occur in pairs. For example, look forward to does not have a monolexical (single-worded) counterpart.

These are the 10 Parts of Speech in alphabetical order:

  1. adjectives (good food)

  2. adverbs (well done)

  3. articles (the right choice)

  4. conjunctions (He's in Europe, but I'm in Africa.)

  5. interjections (Wow! Do you mean that?)

  6. nouns (high quality)

  7. participles (an extended period of time • an interesting book)

  8. prepositions (at home)

  9. pronouns (It's them!)

  10. verbs (We meet often.)

page 4

There are also a handful of words, usually less than 10, called particles, which do not fit into any of the definitions of the Parts of Speech. For example, in English, the word to, when part of the infinitive, is sometimes, but not always, a particle.

Mostly directly, but also indirectly, Parts of Speech are noun-centered, that is nouns are the sun of the solar system of the Parts of Speech. All other 9 Parts of Speech revolve around nouns.

  1. Nouns are names.

    George, Helen, apple, kindness, cruelty, man, horse, tree, gravity are all nouns.

  2. Articles qualify nouns in terms of gender, person, number et al. Some languages lack articles.

    the, a/an, of are all articles.

  3. Adjectives are words that qualify nouns.

    good, bad, heavy, important, ridiculous, this, these are all adjectives.

  1. Participles derive from verbs and, just like adjectives, they qualify nouns. interesting, concerned, complicated, living, are all participles.

  2. Pronouns replace nouns.

    he, she, it, them, herself, this, those are all pronouns.

  3. Verbs denote an action, occurrence, or state of being, in which protagonsists are nouns or pronouns.

    go, put, write, sleep, be, think, work, give are all verbs.

  4. Prepositions answer questions about nouns by qualifying time, place, manner, degree, quantity, quality et al.

    at, in, around, on, under are all prepositions.

  5. Adverbs qualify (a)verbs, (b)adjectives, and (c)other adverbs.

    well, fortunately, sadly, fast, hard are all adverbs.

  6. Conjunctions connect clauses while, at the same time, they establish fundamental relationships between them.

    and, or, therefore, for, while, before are all conjunctions.

  7. Interjections are standalone words or phrases which express strong emotions, sudden reactions, or short exclamations.

    Wow! Ouch! Oh no! Yay! Oops! Ah... Ugh! are all interjections.

The same word can be more than one Part of Speech depending on its function in the clause or phrase. This phenomenon is language dependent.

  1. Some nouns can also be adjectives.

    This animal is beautiful. (noun)
    Animal feed can be expensive. (adjective)

  2. Some adjectives can also be nouns or adverbs.

    This is a good job. (adjective)
    I am thinking of the greater good. (noun)
    She runs fast. (adverb)

  3. Some present/active participles can also be nouns.

    The children are swimming. (present/active participle)

    Swimmimg is a great sport. (noun/gerund)

  4. Some past participles can also be adjectives.

    This issue has always complicated things. (past participle)
    This is a complicated situation. (adjective)

  5. Some pronouns can also be adjectives.

    This is beautiful. (demonstrative pronoun)
    This flower is beautiful. (demonstrative adjective)

  6. Some verbs can also be nouns.

    I sleep well. (verb)
    Adequate sleep is crucial for overall health. (noun)

  7. Some prepositions can also be conjunctions or adverbs.

    He is standing before her. (preposition)
    He had called before she arrived. (conjunction)
    She has done that before. (adverb)

  8. Some adverbs can also be adjectives.

    We work hard. (adverb)
    The soil is very hard. (adjective)

  9. Some conjunctions can also be prepositions.

    She stayed home for, as she said, she was feeling unwell. (conjunction)
    I am doing this for you. (preposition)

A LITTLE HISTORY

Plato, in Plato's Cratylus, and, subsequently, his student, Aristotle, were the first known individuals to have formulated the concept of The Parts of Speech. But it was Dionysius the Thrax, an ancient Greek grammarian and scholar who lived during the 2nd century BC, who spelled out 9 of today's 10 Parts of Speech, leaving out intejections. He is known for his work on grammar, specifically his treatise called "The Art of Grammar." (τέχνη γραμματική)

This work became one of the foundational texts in the field of Greek grammar and had a significant influence on subsequent grammatical studies in both Greek and Latin, and it is still regarded as the groundwork of the entire Western grammatical tradition and beyond.

In "The Art of Grammar," Dionysius the Thrax described the parts of speech in Greek including onomata (nouns and adjectives), verbs, participles, articles, pronouns, prepositions, adverbs, and conjunctions. He also discussed topics such as syntax (sentence structure), and morphology (word formation).

Dionysius the Thrax's work has had a lasting impact on the study of grammar worldwide. His classification of the Parts of Speech and his grammatical analysis methods became fundamental principles in subsequent grammatical studies and influenced the development of grammatical theories in most languages.

The Parts of Speech and their definitions are universal, which means that all languages have exactly the same Parts of Speech. However, grammarians are notoriously inaccurate and will tell you that different languages have different Parts of Speech, which is totally untrue.

What they really mean is that Parts of Speech in different languages may consist of different or more or fewer types or classes or subcategories. For example, they will tell you that   English has 1 class of  adjectives while

page 6

Japanese has 3.

It is true that some languages, such as Russian, lack articles, but the remaining Parts of Speech are the same ones as in every other language.

But why are The Parts of Speech important?

Because each one of them defines a distinct role/function of words in clauses and, because of that, they help us study and learn languages infinitely more efficiently.

The definition of each Part of Speech spells out the role/function of each Part of Speech in a clause and those definitions are formulated on the basis of universal conceptual criteria. That means that if a word satisfies the definition of verbs in a given clause, then it is a verb regardless of language, puncuation, syntax, periphrasis, or morphology. (axiom on page 2)

In clauses or phrases*, words as well as groups of words can be identified on two levels, (a) the lexical level (as a part of speach) and (b) syntactical level (word order).

Let us take a look at this sentence:

Both of them work hard. Parts of Speech or Lexical Analysis:

  • both (pronoun / stedord)

  • of (partitive article / delingsartikel)

  • them (pronoun / stedord)

  • work (verb / udsagnsord)

  • hard (adverb / biord)

Syntactical Analysis:

  • both of them (subject / subjekt)

  • work hard (predicate / udsagnsled)

* A phrase is a word or a group of words that conveys a meaning but does not have the subject-verb unit.

For example:

  • Good!

  • Weather permitting.

  • Once upon a time.

  • God willing!

  • Damn it!

are all phrases.

A WORD ON DEFINITIONS • EN KOMMENTER OM DEFINITIONER

Remember that definitions are not formulated just to waste paper and ink but rather to be religiously observed.

If definitions are not observed, their very purpose is defeated and chaos ensues.

Ridiculously, in the overwheling majority of grammar books that I have come across, authors repeatedly ignore their own definitions, which betrays their mechanical or robotic attitude towards authoring.


This is the top part of page 3 of a Danish grammar "Skriverguiden" titled "At Skrive" authored by Fanny Slotorub. It is a guide to another book by the same title and author on "Tekstsammenhæng og argumentation," ISBN 978-87-636-0418-5, published by "Praxis."

It is written in Danish and the author defines a main clause as a clause that can stand on its own, which is the correct definition of a main clause, citing as examples 2 clauses: Vi spiser. / For vi er sultne. (We eat. / For we are hungry.), for here meaning because.

Here, the author ignores her own definition since For vi er sultne/For we are hungrry cannot stand alone and, therefore, by definition, it does not qualify for a main clause.

Actually, ... for vir er sultne / ... for we are hungry is a subordinate clause, not just in English and Danish, but in all languages.

But what exactly can stand on its own mean in the definition of main clauses?

It means that no additional information is necessary for a clause to make sense.

Let us consider these 2 dialogs:

dialog 1

— Good morning! For we are hungry.

— What!?

dialog 2

page 8

— Good morning! We are hungry.

— Well, make some breakfast.

It is obvious that For we are hungry cannot stand alone and therefore it is a subordinate clause. Period!

So, what is going on?

Let me explain.

In Danish, the syntax of main clauses happens to be Subject + Verb + Adverb or SVA.

For instance, 31 ViS arbejderV hårdt.A / WeS workV hard.A

Now, the syntax of clauses introduced by for also happens to be SVA, that is the same syntax as that of main clauses.

For instance, 32 For vejretS erV ogsåA dårligt. / For the weatherS isV alsoA bad.

Voilà! The incredibly superficial logic emerges! It goes like this:

Since the syntax of the clauses introduced by for is the same as the syntax of main clauses, it follows that clauses introduced by for are also main clauses.

Never mind that clause 32 cannot stand alone, which is the opposite of the very definition of main clauses the author herself cites on page 3 in her own book. That is all forgotten now, which betrays the mechanical or robotic mindset of most authors. The author's logic would be OK if the definition of main clauses were Main clauses are clauses whose syntax is SVA, which, of course, is not the definition of main clauses.

To correct the contradiction, hovedsætningskonjuktioner / main-clause conjunctions should be renamed SVA conjunctions. The rule, then, would be:

The syntax of all subordinate clauses introduced by og, men, eller, for, så is SVA.

What is a clause? What is a main/independent clause? What is a subordinate/dependent clause?

First, what is a clause?

def. 1 A clause is a word or group of words that (a)has a subject and a (b)a verb.

What is a main clause?

From Plato to Aristotle to Dionysius the Thrax to Noam Chomsky, def. 2 a main clause is a clause that (c)can stand on its own (a clause can stand on its own only if it does not contain a conjunction).

We see that 3 requirements have to be satisfied simultaneously for a clause to qualify as a main one.

33 Eat! (imperative mood / bydemåde • imperativ)

Example 33 is a main clause because it has (a)a subject = you, (b)a verb = eat, and (c)it can stand on its own.

34 Jill wakes up early every morning.

Example 34 is a main clause because it has (a)a subject = Jill, (b) a verb = wakes up, and (c)it can stand on its own.

What is a subordinate clause?

def. 3 A subordinate clause is a clause that cannot stand on its own (a clause cannot stand on its if it contains a conjunction).

We see that 3 requirements have to be satisfied simultaneously for a clause to qualify as a subordinate one.

35 And he knows the truth!.

Example 35 is a subordinate clause because it has (a)a subject = he, (b)a verb = knows, and (c)it cannot stand on its own (it is introduced by the conjunction and). 36 Although he dropped out of school.

Example 36 is a subordinate clause because it has (a)a subject = he, (b)a verb = dropped out of, and (c)it cannot stand on its own (it is introduced by the conjunction although).

To summarize, all clauses must have a subject and a verb, but main clauses cannot contain a conjunction while subordinate clauses must contain a conjunction.
